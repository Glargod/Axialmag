2. Theoretical Background

2.1 Magnetostatics and Permanent Magnet Forces

Permanent magnets, such as N52-grade neodymium (NdFeB) with remanence Br ≈ 1.45 T, produce static magnetic fields from aligned atomic dipoles. The force between two axial-magnetized discs is governed by dipole-dipole interactions:

F ≈ (μ₀ / 4π) × (2 m₁ m₂ / r⁴) (for like poles repulsion at distance r)

where μ₀ is vacuum permeability, m₁ and m₂ are magnetic moments (m = Br × volume / μ₀). For thin discs at close gaps, empirical fits show steep decay: F \~ 1/gap² to 1/gap⁴. Repulsion is forced-positive (unidirectional, non-negative), aligning with the concept's no-abs/no-sign-flip rule to reflect mechanical reality (e.g., no negative torque from unilateral contacts).

2.2 Conservative Fields in Dynamic Systems

Magnetic fields from permanent magnets are conservative: forces derive from a position-dependent potential U(r, θ), with net work over closed paths ∫ F · dr = 0. Motion introduces time-varying configurations but remains quasi-static at low speeds, without non-conservative terms like induced emfs (absent conductors). This implies net torque over full cycles averages to zero, challenging sustained rotation claims.

2.3 Cam-Piston Mechanics

The sinusoidal cam converts axial piston forces to rotary torque: τ = F_axial × (dh/dφ), where h(φ) = A sin(3φ) (A = 25 mm amplitude for 50 mm travel, 3 cycles/rev). Slope dh/dφ peaks at 0.075 m/rad, providing mechanical advantage during force windows.

3. System Design

3.1 Overall Architecture

AxialMag consists of a central rotating cam (150 mm radius) driving 7 equidistant pistons (51.4° spacing). Each piston reciprocates axially (50 mm stroke) against fixed or counter-phased profiled plate magnets. The armature (static) holds piston rails, bearings, and enclosure (\~4 L total volume target, car-battery-sized).

  • Cam Profile: Sinusoidal, 3 cycles/rev, ensuring phased overlaps.

  • Ferrous Elements: Optional backing plates (1–2 mm steel) for 1.5–2.5× force boost; parasitic shielding (mu-metal) to reduce crosstalk.

3.2 Gating and Phasing Mechanism

Per piston cycle (120° mechanical): - Attract phase (\~7% pre-hump): Bottom N-S junction pulls piston toward TDC. - Dead zone (TDC, 3 mm gap): Net force near-zero (field cancellation). - Repulse phase (\~7% post-hump): Top like-pole repulsion pushes piston down. - N-S junction: Pole transition, resetting for next cycle. With 7 pistons, overlaps aim for \~85–90% power band coverage, minimizing ripple.

6. Challenges and Objections

Despite clever gating, conservative fields ensure net zero work over cycles: Attract release balances repulse store. Motion unsettles flux but doesn't create non-conservative gains. Losses (friction, eddies) cause decay to stop, not "centuries-long" graceful halt (no radioisotope-like depletion in PMs).

Assembly challenges: Imbalance torques require locked axle, adjustable jigs. No verified passive PM systems sustain rotation; prototypes stop quickly.

7. Conclusion

AxialMag pushes magnetic-mechanical boundaries with phased gating and positivity rules, potentially achieving brief rotation from imbalances. However, conservation laws limit sustained output. Prototyping with adjustability and measurements is recommended to validate or refine. Future work: FEM simulations, ferrous optimizations, dyno testing.
